Introduction to Disciplines of Social Sciences

Definition of Social Sciences

The social sciences study society, human behavior, and the forces shaping social interactions. Key disciplines include:

  1. Anthropology - Examines ancient cultures and human evolution.
  2. Demography - Analyzes population size, structure, and trends.
  3. Economics - Focuses on resource allocation to meet unlimited human needs.
  4. Geography - Studies human-environment interactions and spatial patterns.
  5. History - Systematically investigates past events to understand their impact.
  6. Linguistics - Explores language structure, acquisition, and cultural significance.
  7. Political Science - Analyzes power, governance, and political systems.
  8. Psychology - Investigates mental processes and behavior.
  9. Sociology - Studies social relationships, institutions, and collective behavior.

Social Sciences vs. Natural Sciences vs. Humanities

  1. Social Sciences - Study human societies and interactions (e.g., economics, sociology).
  2. Natural Sciences - Examine natural phenomena (e.g., physics, biology).
  3. Humanities - Focus on human expression and philosophy (e.g., literature, art).
  4. Similarity - All use research methods to explain phenomena, but differ in focus (tangible vs. intangible).

Historical Context of Key Disciplines

  1. Anthropology - Emerged from early travel accounts (e.g., Herodotus) and Enlightenment studies.
  2. Economics - Developed during the Enlightenment (Adam Smith’s Wealth of Nations).
  3. Geography - Pioneered by Eratosthenes ("Father of Geography").
  4. History - Refined by Herodotus and Thucydides using critical methods.
  5. Linguistics - Evolved from philosophy to a scientific study of language (e.g., Saussure).
  6. Political Science - Rooted in Aristotle’s Politics; formalized post-WWII.
  7. Psychology - Became experimental with Wilhelm Wundt.
  8. Sociology - Born from Industrial Revolution challenges (e.g., Auguste Comte).

Importance of Social Sciences

  1. Future Planning - Guides ethical and social responses to technological advances.
  2. Economic Understanding - Helps navigate crises (e.g., recessions).
  3. Safety and Rights - Improves policies on human dignity and crowd control.
  4. Global Betterment - Addresses inequality and promotes social justice.
  5. Personal Enrichment - Offers new perspectives on culture and daily life.

Module 2

Philippine Politics & Governance

Politics: Arena vs Process

Arena Approach - Focuses on formal political institutions like the state, laws, and policies. It views politics as the "authoritative allocation of values" (Easton, 1957).

Process Approach - Extends beyond government to include institutions like unions, corporations, and NGOs that influence societal rules.

Definition of Politics

Defined as “the authoritative allocation of values for a society” (1953) and the study of politics as “understanding how authoritative decisions are made and executed for a society.” (1957).

Politics as About Governing

Government is the term used to describe formal institutions (groups of people that are ruled or governed). It extends to include the persons and organizations that make, enforce political decisions for society. Also included are non-government organizations (trade unions, corporations, NGOs) that influence rules and member behavior.

Politics as Governance

Governance is broader than government, encompassing public and private systems that organize societal, economic, and political life. It involves formal/informal rules, norms, and institutions (from local to global) that govern behavior.

Functions of Politics

  1. Inputs (Demands & Supports): Public needs (jobs, healthcare) and enabling conditions (laws, public trust).
  2. Outputs (Decisions & Policies): Government responses to societal demands.

Politics as “Who Gets, What, When, and How”

Power and the Three Faces of Power

  1. Decision-Making - Who wins in policy conflicts.
  2. Agenda-Setting - Controlling which issues are debated.
  3. Preference-Shaping - Influencing public beliefs (e.g., ideological hegemony).

Political Ideologies

An ideology is a more or less coherent set of ideas that provides the basis for organized political action. All ideologies offer an account of the existing order.

Five Major Political Ideologies

  1. Anarchism
    • View of the state: rejects the state as entirely oppressive
    • Core beliefs: advocates spontaneous, stateless order
  2. Socialism
    • View of the state: as a tool for equality
    • Core beliefs: collectivism, social justice, public welfare
  3. Liberalism
    • View of the state: as a neutral arbiter
    • Core beliefs: individual freedom, progress, diversity
  4. Conservatism
    • View of the state: strong state for order
    • Core beliefs: hierarchy, tradition, skepticism of change
  5. Fascism
    • View of the state: totalitarian state
    • Core beliefs: elite rule, nationalism, anti-equality, freedom as submission to authoritarian rule

States, Nations, and Globalization

  1. Nation - A cultural group with shared language and history.
  2. State - A political entity which provides its citizens protection from internal and external insecurity and aggression.
    • People - Citizens
    • Territory - Defined borders
    • Government - Ruling institutions
    • Sovereignty - Supreme authority
  3. Globalization - Erosion of borders through trade, technology, and cultural exchange.
  4. Economic Globalization - Characterized by the internationalization of markets, trade, finance, investment.
