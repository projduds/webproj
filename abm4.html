INTRODUCTION OF BUSINESS ETHICS

THE ROLE OF BUSINESS IN SOCIAL AND ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT

What is Business?
An organization that exchanges goods and services for one another or money. Each business requires a steady customer base and form of investment to generate profit. Businesses can be privately owned, nonprofit, or state-run.

Basic Forms of Business Organizations

1. Sole Proprietorship

Owned and operated by one person. Full control and all profits, but also complete responsibility for liabilities.

  • Advantages:
    • Easy and low-cost to set up.
    • Full authority to decision-making.
    • Profits taxed as personal income.
    • Simple to close if needed.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Personal financial risk.
    • Difficulty securing funding.
    • Harder to attract skilled employees.
    • Limited tax deductions for certain owner benefits.

2. Partnership

Owned and operated by two or more individuals. Formal agreement on profit, decision-making and conflict resolution.

  • Advantages:
    • Combines skills and expertise.
    • Simple to establish.
    • Greater access to capital.
    • Profits pass to partners' tax returns.
    • Can attract talent with partnership opportunities.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Potential conflicts in decision-making.
    • Joint and individual liability.
    • Tax disadvantages for employee benefits.
    • Limited continuity if partners leave.
    • Required profit sharing reduces earnings.

3. Corporation

Separate legal entity from owners (shareholders). Provides strong liability protection.

  • Advantages:
    • Limited liability for shareholders.
    • Strong ability to raise capital.
    • Continuous existence.
    • Tax-deductible employee benefits.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Expensive and time-consuming incorporation.
    • Extensive oversight and paperwork.
    • Potential for double taxation.
    • Less flexibility in management.

4. Cooperative

Member-owned with equally distributed control and benefits.

  • Advantages:
    • Democratic governance.
    • Limited liability protection.
    • Profit distribution based on participation.
    • Lower registration costs.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Slow decision-making.
    • Requires active participation.
    • Limited appeal to investors.
    • Complex record-keeping.
    • Potential internal conflicts.

Basic Classifications of Business

  • Service Businesses: Provide intangible products like skills, expertise, or labor.
  • Merchandising Businesses: Buy and resell finished products without altering them.
  • Manufacturing Businesses: Convert raw materials into finished products.

Why Start a Business?

Core Principles in Business Operations

1. Fairness

Impartial and equitable treatment in decisions. Emphasizes judgment free from bias.

  • Principles of Justice: Equal treatment unless justified by differences.
  • Kinds of Justice:
    • Distributive - Fair benefit/burden allocation.
    • Retributive - Punishment for wrongdoing.
    • Compensatory - Compensation for harm.

2. Accountability

Responsibility for actions and open reporting to stakeholders.

3. Transparency

Operating without hidden agendas to foster trust.

4. Stewardship

Leaders’ duty to preserve/enhance organizations for future successors.

Business Practice

  • Decorum: Socially acceptable professional behavior.
  • Protocol: Unwritten rules for business interactions.
  • Policies: Guidelines for decision-making.
  • Advertising: Promoting products/services.
  • Marketing: Understanding and meeting customer needs.
  • Bookkeeping: Recording financial transactions.
  • Reportorial Requirements:
    • Annual Report
    • Financial Statements: Balance Sheets, Income Statements, Cash Flow Reports
  • Documentation: Evidence for business actions and decisions.

INTRODUCTION TO BUSINESS FINANCE

Finance

Financial management is the art and science of managing money to maximize shareholder wealth. Its primary goal is to increase the company's stock value through strategic decisions about investments, financing, and operations. Financial risk management is crucial as unexpected events can lead to financial losses.

Corporate Structure & Responsibilities

  • Shareholders: Owners who elect the Board of Directors (1 share = 1 vote)
  • Board of Directors: Top policy-makers who ensure the company serves shareholders' interests by:
    • Setting investment and dividend policies
    • Approving strategies and budgets
    • Appointing/removing top management
    • Determining executive compensation
    • Reviewing financial disclosures
  • President/CEO: Oversees operations and implements board-approved strategies while representing the company publicly.
  • Vice Presidents:
    • Marketing: Develops sales strategies, analyzes competition, and manages customer relationships
    • Production: Optimizes manufacturing processes, minimizes costs, and manages supply chains
    • Administration: Coordinates departments, manages HR/office needs, and reduces operating costs
    • Finance: Determines optimal capital structure (debt vs. equity mix) and manages:
      • Financing decisions (long-term investments and working capital)
      • Investing decisions (fund allocation)
      • Operating decisions (daily financial operations)
      • Dividend policies (profit distribution to shareholders)

Key Financial Concepts

  • Principle 1: Time Value of Money – Money today is worth more than the same amount in the future due to its earning potential.
  • Principle 2: Risk-Return Tradeoff – Higher-risk investments require higher returns to attract investors.
  • Principle 3: Cash Flows are the Source of Value – Real cash inflows/outflows reflect a business’s financial health, not accounting profits.
  • Principle 4: Market Efficiency – Developed markets adjust quickly to new information, making it hard to profit from public data.
  • Principle 5: Incentives Drive Individuals – Misaligned incentives between managers and owners can reduce firm value.

Forms of Business

By Nature/Purpose:

  • Service: Personal/professional services (e.g., consulting)
  • Trading: Buying/selling products (e.g., retail)
  • Manufacturing: Converting raw materials into finished goods
  • Banking/Finance: Money and credit services
  • Mining: Extracting natural resources (e.g., oil, metals)
  • Construction: Building infrastructure
  • Genetic: Breeding plants/animals for sale or reproduction

By Ownership:

  1. Sole Proprietorship
    Pros: Easy to start, full control, minimal capital.
    Cons: Unlimited liability, limited growth, short lifespan.
  2. Partnership
    Pros: Shared resources/skills, flexible formation.
    Cons: Unlimited liability (for general partners), dissolves easily.
  3. Corporation
    Pros: Limited liability, perpetual existence, easy capital raising.
    Cons: Complex setup, double taxation, agency problems.
  4. Cooperative
    Pros: Democratic governance, social mission, perpetual existence.
    Cons: Limited funding, low financial returns, management gaps.

The Role of Money

Money serves two key functions:

  • Medium of Exchange: Accepted for payments (goods/services/debts). Historically, items like shells and metals served as money.
  • Store of Value: Preserves purchasing power over time (though assets like stocks or real estate can also serve this purpose).

Financial Institutions

A. Banks

  • Thrift Banks: Serve rural areas with consumer credit and deposits.
  • Commercial Banks: Offer checking/savings accounts and lend to SMEs.
  • Universal Banks: Handle large, multinational transactions in multiple currencies.
  • Investment Banks: Raise capital for corporations/governments via bonds and IPOs.

B. Nonbanks

  • Leasing/Investment Companies: Provide credit but aren’t banks.
  • Mutual Funds: Pool small investments for collective management.
  • Insurance Companies: Offer coverage for premiums, managed by portfolio managers.
  • Private Equity Funds: Unregulated entities investing in private companies.

Financial Instruments

  • Financial Assets: Cash, stocks, receivables, or rights to favorable exchanges (e.g., bonds).
  • Financial Liabilities: Obligations to pay cash or exchange assets unfavorably (e.g., loans payable).
  • Equity Instruments: Represent ownership (e.g., common/preferred stock).
  • Debt Instruments: (e.g., treasury bonds) offer fixed returns; equity (e.g., stocks) varies with company performance.

Financial Markets

  • Primary vs. Secondary Markets:
    • Primary: New securities issued (e.g., IPOs).
    • Secondary: Trading of existing securities (e.g., stock exchanges).
  • Money vs. Capital Markets:
    • Money Markets: Short-term securities (1 year).
    • Capital Markets: Long-term securities (stocks/bonds).
